Takeover Guide (Last updated on March 7th 2023)

Part 0: Initiation

So, you want to learn how to play Astatos? If so, you've come to the right place!
In this tutorial, I, Max, will guide you through the basics of this game's PvP mode: Takeover!
As soon as you start an online game, (Through the Trial -> Takeover menu) you'll have to pick Heroes to ban (1 per player) and pick Heroes to use. (5 per player) Only then can the game really start.
After a short loading screen, you'll have to pick between two cards; These determine the side you're on, (Makari-Hari or Mukaki) But we'll get to that later.
Then, you'll be given four cards, which you can select to reroll or keep.
You'll have to wait for your opponent to finish their hand, and then...

Part I: The Trial Begins

After taking part in the starting trial, you most likely played a card (or two!) and got energy from it. That's great! But what can you use it for?
Well, energy has a few uses, but they're all centered on one mechanic: Heroes! These cards have different effects (see: Card Reference), and are part of the win condition for Takeover: Influence!
Your influence has two indicators: The bar at the top, and the card displayed in the lower left of the screen, after you reach 5 influence points for your side, you win!
I've been going on about influence, but how do you even get it? As I (somewhat) said before, you use different heroes' abilities, of course! Most of them are fairly simple, like drawing a card (Cerkles) or removing one energy from your opponent (Herclaus)! However! There are a few cards with some special features, leading us to...

Part II: The God and The Devil

Now, you may have noticed that you and your opponent already have one card summoned; These are Makari-Hari and Mukaki.
They both have two abilities; one active and one passive, which can turn the tide of the game in your favor! Let's take a look at them:

Overview

Unlike most heroes, both Makari-Hari and Mukaki's active ability REMOVE one influence from your side, so be wary of that! (Note: If any of them is flipped to the opponent's side, they will be unable to use any abilities, active or passive)

Makari-Hari

Makari-Hari's passive is fairly simple, simply drawing another card when you play a spell card; Nothing to write home about.
His active ability is also simple: "Lose one influence and obtain one Energy per Card held by Mukaki's side.", often used as a counter to Mukaki's ability, at least depending on the amount of cards you have
Speaking of which...

Mukaki

Mukaki's ability is still simple: "Add one cooldown to Makari-Hari, and swap all cards with your opponent." However, it proves to be a strong asset in your favor, considering that you can easily run out of cards as Mukaki, tying in to his passive: "Players on Mukaki's side can play up to two Cards during the Trial Stage."

In short...

Both of their abilities are crucial to getting an edge on your opponent and winning, but since they both have a long 5 turn cooldown, using their abilities when the time is right is incredibly important to win!

Part III: Disparage and Exchange

Overview

Disparity and Exchange are relatively new mechanics, both meant as a way to come back from bad RNG or other causes. Let's take a look at them:

Disparity

When the game starts, both you and your opponent start off with an empty disparity meter, which only gains one charge when the respective side skips a trial.
As of writing this guide (March 1st, 2023), there is only one action you can preform: Disparage, which doubles every influence you gain from your own hero abilities.
Funnily enough, it's mainly used to finish a game, rather than to stay afloat. (Speaking from personal experience, at least)

Exchange

A very straightforward mechanic, exchange three cards, or two when using Gaios, and gain one energy. Extremly useful to stay afloat when you have bad RNG or need to use a hero ability and have no energy left! (Note: You cannot preform an exchange if you start your turn with two or more Energy)
It does have its flaws however. Mainly the fact that it depends on cards, and if your opponent can consistently remove cards from your hand (eg: Krokos, Regulus, Caelius, etc.) you're screwed, to put it lightly. Overall, it's a balanced mechanic, not too much to say about it
